    Wet/Dry Cycling Device for David Deamer's Research in Non-Enzymatic RNA Synthesis

    The Device in Action

    Project Poster

    Project Origin and Leadership

    This project began when I reached out to Professor David Deamer, a pioneer in origins-of-life research, asking if he had a problem suitable for a team of robotics engineers. He challenged us to design an automated wet/dry cycling device to simulate prebiotic hydrothermal pools—conditions believed to be crucial for RNA polymerization. I then formally proposed the concept to my class, chose a team of five engineers from a pool of applicants, and served as both technical lead and project manager.

    Over the course of the year, I managed the full engineering cycle—from brainstorming and subsystem design, to integration, testing, and delivery. I also worked closely with Professor Deamer and his postdoc, Vikas Peddu, to ensure the final system could be immediately deployed in their lab for real experiments.

    System Architecture

    The Wet/Dry Cycler integrates multiple subsystems: dehydration, rehydration, mixing, gas delivery, sample extraction, embedded control, and a web-based GUI. At the heart of the device is an ESP32-S3 microcontroller orchestrating all subsystems via a finite state machine, with real-time monitoring and control through a React/Node.js web interface.

    Subsystem Engineering

    Heating: A 12V/200W silicone pad bonded to a 2024 aluminum plate provided uniform heating. Temperature feedback was regulated with bang-bang control to maintain ±2 °C.

    Rehydration: A custom syringe pump with NEMA-17 stepper and 13-port PTFE manifold dispensed ~100 µL water per cycle with ±13% precision. Tubing resistance was tuned to equalize flow.

    Mixing: Magnetic stir bars driven by DC motors enabled independent mixing of three vial zones. MOSFET drivers allowed precise on/off control.

    Gas Delivery: A manifold distributed CO₂ or N₂ across vial zones, supporting dehydration and atmosphere control.

    Sample Extraction: The vial plate moved via stepper-driven linear motion into an extraction zone, where pipettes accessed samples through silicone plugs.

    Electronics & PCB: A custom PCB consolidated power distribution, MOSFET switching, DRV8825 drivers, and signal routing. Following IPC-2221 standards, it ensured reliability under high current loads.

    Embedded Control: ESP32 firmware ran a robust finite state machine (IDLE → REHYDRATING → MIXING → HEATING → EXTRACTION). Communication with the front end was maintained via WebSockets.

    User Interface: A React-based GUI allowed researchers to configure cycles, monitor progress, and log data. Recovery state JSON handling ensured resilience to disconnections.

    Device Design and Implementation

    Delivery, Training, and User Manual

    Beyond building the device, we provided hands-on training sessions in Professor Deamer's Research Lab, going through setup, operation, and troubleshooting. A detailed User Guide documents step-by-step instructions for powering, networking, priming, experiment setup, and error handling.

    This ensured that the Wet/Dry Cycler was not just a prototype, but a usable tool for ongoing origins-of-life research in Professor Deamer's group.

    Final Project Report

    Leadership and Takeaways

    Leading this project was as much about engineering leadership as technical design. Managing a five-person team taught me how to:

    • Delegate subsystem ownership while maintaining system-wide integration.
    • Balance technical rigor with timeline and budget constraints.
    • Communicate with stakeholders (faculty, postdocs, classmates) and align priorities.
    • Document engineering work according to professional standards (IPC, ANSI, IEEE).

    By the end of the project, I had gained invaluable experience in both system engineering and team management, while delivering a full-fledged product that is now actively supporting experimental RNA synthesis research.
